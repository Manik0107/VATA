## Core Concepts

Narrator: So, what *is* Linear Regression? Imagine you're planting seeds. [ANIMATE: Seeds being planted, growing into plants of different heights] Some seeds get more sunlight, some get more water. Linear Regression helps us figure out how much the sunlight and water (our explanatory variables) affect how tall the plants grow (our response variable).

Alex: Explanatory and response variables? Sounds like a detective show!

Narrator: Exactly! The **explanatory variable** is what we *use* to make a prediction – like the amount of sunlight. [HIGHLIGHT: explanatory variable] And the **response variable** is what we're trying to *predict* – like the plant's height. [HIGHLIGHT: response variable] [SHORT PAUSE] The explanatory variable usually goes on the x-axis (horizontal) and the response variable on the y-axis (vertical).

Mia: So, if I know how much sunlight a plant gets, I can guess how tall it will be?

Narrator: Precisely! Linear Regression draws a line through the data points on a graph to show the relationship between the two variables. [DISPLAY DIAGRAM: Scatterplot with a regression line] This line helps us predict!

## Mathematical Derivations

Narrator: Okay, let’s get a *little* bit mathematical, but don't worry, it's not scary! This line we draw is called the regression line, and it has a super-important equation: y equals a plus b x. [HIGHLIGHT: y = a + bx]

Alex: Whoa, that looks like alien math!

Narrator: It's simpler than it looks, Alex! In this equation, "y" is the predicted value of the response variable, like the plant's height. [ANIMATE: 'y' highlighting on equation, then a plant growing] "x" is the value of the explanatory variable, like the amount of sunlight. [ANIMATE: 'x' highlighting, then a sun icon appearing]

Mia: Okay, I think I get that. What about "a" and "b"?

Narrator: Great question, Mia! "a" is the intercept – the value of y when x is zero. Think of it as the plant's height if it got *no* sunlight. And "b" is the slope – it tells us how much y changes for every one-unit increase in x. So, if "b" is 2, for every extra hour of sunlight, the plant grows 2 centimeters taller! [DISPLAY DIAGRAM: Graph of equation y=a+bx, labeling intercept and slope] [MEDIUM PAUSE] The computer finds the best "a" and "b" to make the line as close as possible to all the data points.

## Examples

Narrator: Let's say we have some data on students' study hours and their test scores. [DISPLAY IMAGE: Table of study hours and test scores] We use Linear Regression and find that the equation is: test score equals 50 plus 5 times study hours.

Alex: So, if a student studies for 2 hours, what's their predicted score?

Narrator: Exactly! Test score equals 50 plus 5 times 2, which equals 60! So, we predict they'll get a score of 60. [ANIMATE: Calculation of test score]

Mia: That’s pretty cool! But what if their actual score is different?

Narrator: Excellent point, Mia! The difference between the predicted score and the actual score is called a residual. [HIGHLIGHT: residual] It tells us how far off our prediction was.
